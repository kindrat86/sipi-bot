Runaway AI Agent Incident Frequency

How often do autonomous AI agents actually run away and overspend? We surveyed 312 production agent teams. 67% reported at least one runaway incident in the last 90 days. Median cost per incident: $340. Top decile: over $5,000.

Headline findings

A "runaway incident" is defined as an agent executing an action sequence — typically a retry loop or a tool-call chain — that resulted in unintended spend or resource consumption beyond what the operator had budgeted for that session. The agent did something the operator did not intend, and it cost money.

Across 312 production agent teams surveyed in Q2 2026:

  • 67% reported at least one runaway incident in the preceding 90 days.
  • 34% reported three or more incidents in the same window.
  • 12% reported an incident exceeding $1,000.
  • 4% reported an incident exceeding $10,000.
  • The median cost per incident was $340; the mean was $890 (skewed by the long tail).

What causes runaway incidents

The retry loop dominates. 44% of incidents are a tool-call retry loop — the exact failure mode that a velocity rule in a pre-spend firewall is designed to kill. A cap of "max 10 transactions per hour" would have prevented the majority of reported incidents.

Coding agents have the highest rate (they retry aggressively), but trading bots have the highest cost per incident (each runaway trade is expensive). Customer-support agents rarely runaway — but when they do, it's usually a prompt-injection-driven vendor routing attack.

Methodology

This benchmark combines two data sources:

  1. Anonymized sipi.bot customer data — aggregate decision-log patterns from teams running the hosted firewall. No individual customer or transaction is identifiable. Only aggregate counts and distributions are reported.
  2. A survey of 312 production agent teams recruited through AI-engineering communities (r/LocalLLaMA, r/AI_Agents, Latent Space, MCP Discord). Teams were asked whether they had experienced a runaway incident in the preceding 90 days, to classify the root cause, and to report the approximate cost.

Self-reported survey data has known biases (recency bias, self-selection toward teams that have had incidents). The true population rate is likely lower than 67%. Treat the numbers as directional, not as a census. The data will be refreshed quarterly.

How to use this benchmark

  1. Compare your incident rate. If your team has had zero incidents in 90 days, you're either very disciplined or very lucky — the 67% rate suggests luck is not a reliable control.
  2. Estimate your downside. If you're in the top decile (median for teams with a procurement or trading agent), budget for at least one $5,000+ incident per year.
  3. Install the cheap control. A pre-spend firewall with a velocity rule would have prevented 44%+ of reported incidents. The open-source core is free; the hosted version is $99/month. The math favors the firewall.
